A personal essay reflecting on the overwhelming scope of responsibilities placed on a single adult in 21st-century Western society. The author opens with the observation that something is structurally "uncalibrated" — despite unprecedented technological abundance, bookshelves overflow with self-help, phones are blamed rather than celebrated, and therapy has become a mass market.

The core argument proceeds historically: in prior generations, the domestic and economic load was distributed across family and social units. A person might have maintained a handful of close relationships requiring little active maintenance, contributed to one economic role, and lived within a stable, small social world. Now a single person is expected to manage the full stack alone — household upkeep across dozens of recurring tasks, food procurement and cooking and nutrition planning, career acquisition and advancement, workplace performance, commuting, side hustles, exercise, elaborate personal grooming routines, hobbies, romantic relationships, and constant social availability across multiple platforms.

The author ties this to a structural ratchet: productivity gains from technology have not produced the reduction in working hours Keynes anticipated. Instead, employers use efficiency gains to raise the ceiling of expected output. The treadmill accelerates rather than stops.

A personal dimension runs through the piece: the author is a gay man who grew up in the early 2000s, who spent formative time with the adult women of his family rather than in masculine outdoor labour. His reflection on how he would have fit into prior historical arrangements adds texture — "allowed in which pasts?" — without resolving the tension.

The note closes with a structured wish-list: the author wants a comprehensive inventory of everything a human must do to live in the 21st century, organized by necessity tier (survival, maintenance, flourishing) and frequency (daily, weekly, monthly, lifetime), and then prioritized into a realistic schedule. He explicitly flags his tendency toward setting unrealistic expectations that collapse into demotivation, and asks for the bar to be set at achievable rather than aspirational.

This connects to the author's ongoing project of designing an intentional, [[Optimal Life Design]]-oriented framework — wanting not perfection, but the confidence that actions are pointing in the right direction.
