Personal responses to a structured ASD referral questionnaire covering seven domains: social interaction, sensory sensitivities, routines and change, interests, motor skills, emotional regulation, and self-reflection.

**Social interaction.** Structured or topic-driven social situations feel manageable — watching a film and discussing it afterwards, large parties where one can circulate freely or step away unnoticed. Unstructured small-group settings (pub trips, dinners) are the hardest: the absence of a clear conversational object creates sustained anxiety. Maintaining long-term friendships is increasingly difficult as shared novelty diminishes and conversation reverts to formulaic check-ins. [[Social Communication Differences]] are clearest in an inability to read tone, facial expression, or subtext; the questionnaire includes a vivid example of a board-game social cue being missed despite multiple deliberate signals from another person.

**Sensory sensitivity.** A detailed profile emerges: tactile aversion to tight or ill-fitting clothes, clothing tags, velvet, and moss; auditory aversion to chewing, lip-smacking, clinking dinnerware, and fluorescent-light buzz/strobe; visual aversion to bright cold lighting. Possible under-sensitivity to cold: consistently no coat in winter despite others noticing the temperature. See [[Sensory Sensitivity]].

**Routines and change.** No established routine is kept; the idea of scheduling is itself stressful. Unexpected changes split into two reactions — shutdown when the disruption is external/impersonal, anger/irritation when another person initiates the change — with a consistent impulse to abort and reschedule rather than adapt in the moment.

**Interests and focus.** A small set of permanent safe topics (mythology, magic) coexists with intense episodic hyperfixations on new subjects (most recently [[BTS]]). Social awareness of the hyperfixation's impact on others came retrospectively (friends asking to stop BTS conversation after roughly a year). See [[Hyperfixation]].

**Motor skills.** Spatial awareness difficulties noted (frequent bumping and spilling in familiar environments). Repeated failed attempts to learn instruments (flute, guitar, piano) — described as an inability to get fingers to move as intended rather than lack of effort.

**Emotional regulation.** Intense reactions to being wronged or witnessing rudeness, perceived as overreaction by others. Coping strategies described include physical release (screaming, hitting objects) and vengeful fantasy; meditation reported to amplify rather than reduce distress. See [[Emotional Dysregulation]].

**Identity and self-reflection.** A persistent sense of not having received an implicit "human" education that others share. Fluid self-concept: difficulty settling into a stable identity as peers do. Heavy reliance on personality quizzes to understand both self and others — the results felt revelatory while others treated them as trivial. Answers to "what do you want/need?" are inaccessible without lived experience; this creates [[vulnerability to suggestion]] and a pattern of going along until burnout clarifies what is not wanted. Connects to [[Depression and Ideation]], [[Motivational Conflict]], and [[Authentic Wants]].
