A raw, unfiltered processing note written in response to advice that the author already knew was good and already knew wouldn't work.

The problem is not knowledge. The inertial self — the one who refuses — already has all the information. It has heard every argument. It has watched the other self beg. And it still says no. What's being described is not a motivation problem in the usual sense. It is a split: two people inhabiting the same body, one of whom is desperate to change and one of whom, with full awareness of that desperation, refuses. Any system that relies on the refusing self to initiate action will fail. The author knows this. That is part of what makes it so exhausting.

Underneath the immediate frustration is something larger: a loss of faith in humanity as a project worth participating in. Not abstract nihilism, but something earned through attention. The author sees the gap between what people are capable of and what they choose, and cannot locate enough beauty in the collective to balance against the ugliness. Even the people who do choose their higher natures tend to become judgemental — and the author catches themselves doing exactly that, right now, in the act of writing this.

The [[Authentic Wants]] are named directly: building with LLMs and AI, ethical technology, worker co-operatives, being their own boss, teaching, writing. But these feel foreclosed — not as dreams given up lightly, but as a clear-eyed assessment that the structural conditions don't exist. Nobody funds a non-profit trying to take down big tech. That isn't self-pity; it is stated as an honest appraisal of the game. The anger is partly about not getting what you want, but more about the game being rigged.

The class and [[Privilege and Guilt|privilege thread]] is the most knotted part. The author worked extraordinarily hard from a position that included serious disadvantages: Northern Ireland, gay, two decades of trauma, a history of physical and sexual abuse, years of social isolation, a teenage self locked in a bedroom grinding through homework for hours trying to build a future. 13 A* GCSEs against an average of 6. 6 A-levels against an average of 3. Internships, sacrifice, burnout. And then someone with significantly greater material and social privilege called them privileged — correctly, on the axis of race and gender — and they took it to heart completely. They haven't let it go. Now they feel unable to enjoy what they earned without guilt, while watching peers who had far larger head starts carry their success with total ease of conscience.

The note breaks off mid-sentence in the middle of a thought about the person who told them this.
