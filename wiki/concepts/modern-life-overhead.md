Modern life overhead refers to the sprawling set of responsibilities that a single adult in contemporary Western society is expected to manage alone — tasks that in prior generations were distributed across family units, trades, and tight-knit communities. The term captures the structural mismatch between the scale of demands and the capacity of one person.

The domains of overhead broadly include:

- **Household maintenance** — daily, weekly, and monthly cleaning, laundry, organising, and upkeep of an entire living space without division of labour
- **Food** — procurement, budgeting, cooking, nutrition planning, and cleanup, multiple times per day
- **Career** — job acquisition (applications, interviews, competition), sustained performance, workplace relationships, commuting, and continuous self-improvement to avoid displacement
- **Financial management** — budgeting, subscriptions, taxes, savings, investments
- **Health and fitness** — daily exercise, sleep hygiene, medical appointments, mental health
- **Grooming and presentation** — elaborate personal care routines (skincare, haircare, clothing selection) that carry social and professional stakes
- **Relationships** — maintaining family, friendships, and romantic partnerships across in-person contact and multiple digital platforms
- **Self-development** — meditation, journalling, reading, hobby cultivation, side projects
- **Identity and planning** — life direction, values clarification, long-range goal setting, avoiding regret

A key tension: technology has made many of these tasks easier individually, yet the total expected volume has expanded to fill and exceed the time saved. The expectation of constant connectivity, for example, transforms what was geographically-bounded sociality (a few dozen people seen regularly) into a boundless obligation requiring active maintenance across apps and platforms.

The productivity treadmill compounds this: efficiency gains from technology have not reduced working hours as Keynes anticipated. Instead, employers have raised the expected output ceiling, and cultural norms have added side hustles and monetized hobbies as further obligations. The result is a ratchet — each improvement in tools increases what is expected of the person using them.
