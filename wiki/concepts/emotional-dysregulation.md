Emotional dysregulation describes a pattern in which emotional responses are more intense, more difficult to modulate, and slower to resolve than those of neurotypical peers in the same situation.

The intensity is not performance or exaggeration — it is the actual experienced magnitude of the emotion. An injustice that others process and move on from within minutes may sustain the same level of distress for hours. This creates a systematic mismatch between one's internal experience and the social consensus about what a situation "warrants", leading to repeated feedback that a reaction is excessive ("it wasn't that bad", "they didn't mean anything by it", "you need to toughen up").

A useful frame: where others have gradually developed emotional calluses that buffer minor provocations, someone with dysregulation is still experiencing the full, unmediated response each time. This is not immaturity; it is a different baseline reactivity.

Common triggers include perceived injustice (being wronged, witnessing rudeness toward others) and unexpected changes imposed by other people. The latter differs from changes caused by impersonal circumstances — person-initiated changes carry an additional relational charge.

Coping strategies vary widely in social acceptability. Physical discharge (screaming, breaking objects) and escapist fantasy (working through retribution mentally) both achieve resolution through action or release rather than cognitive reappraisal. Mindfulness-based approaches are frequently reported as counterproductive in this profile: turning attention toward the feeling amplifies rather than reduces it, because the goal is change or resolution, not acceptance.

Emotional dysregulation intersects with [[Sensory Sensitivity]] (sensory overload as a trigger), [[Social Communication Differences]] (misreading others' signals can create unnecessary conflict), and [[Depression and Ideation]] (sustained high-intensity emotional reactivity as an exhausting baseline). It is a recognised feature of [[ASD]] presentations, though not exclusive to autism.
