Hyperfixation is an intense, consuming focus on a specific subject, object, or activity, characterised by a drive to learn or engage exhaustively and difficulty directing attention away from it voluntarily.

It differs from ordinary enthusiasm in two ways: the depth of engagement (an urge to learn *everything* rather than a general interest) and the displacement effect (the fixation crowds out other conversational topics and activities, often without the person being aware of the impact on others until informed).

Two modes tend to coexist. A person may maintain one or two stable, enduring interests that function as a reliable safe space — always available, always calming — alongside episodic hyperfixations on new subjects that arrive suddenly and burn intensely before stabilising or fading. The entry into a new fixation often feels like a rush or compulsion rather than a deliberate choice.

The social cost of hyperfixation is frequently invisible to the person experiencing it. The topic feels universally interesting; the amount of time spent on it in conversation does not register as disproportionate. Feedback, when it comes, is often experienced as embarrassing because the social impact was genuinely not perceived in real time.

Hyperfixation appears in both [[ASD]] and ADHD presentations and is considered a feature of [[Social Communication Differences]] — the inability to read others' disengagement or disinterest. It also intersects with [[Authentic Wants]]: stable hyperfixations reveal what genuinely produces aliveness and engagement, independent of external expectation.
