The recurring fantasy of a powerful, wealthy patron who recognises rare intellectual or creative ability and provides protection, resources, and autonomy in exchange for the fruits of that work. The person under patronage gets to pursue their own research; the patron gets prestige, utility, or power from the outputs.

This model has historical precedents: court astronomers, alchemists, scholars apprenticed to lords, shamans under chieftain protection, philosophers sponsored by rulers. The fantasy is compelling because it resolves a specific problem — the person who produces high-value knowledge but cannot survive on the open market alone — without requiring that person to submit to the mundane structures of employment.

## The Fantasy and Its Distortions

The patron model is attractive precisely because it seems to offer a third way between entrepreneurship (exhausting, risky, lonely) and corporate employment (safe, monotonous, constraining). The patron handles resources and protection; the knowledge-worker handles ideas.

But the fantasy has predictable failure modes:
- The patron decides what counts as useful output, not the worker
- Failure to deliver (even an impossible request) risks total dismissal or worse
- The worker's leverage is only as strong as their productive value, with no floor
- Autonomy is granted, not owned — it can be revoked at any time

## Modern Manifestations

In contemporary terms, the patron's castle is the corporation. A salary and equity package is "protection." Relocation support, good equipment, and prestige projects are the court's rewards. The worker believes they are valued; they are valuable. Those are not the same.

The patron model also appears in the startup world's VC-funded founder: the investor as patron, the founder as court intellectual. The same dynamics apply — the investor owns more than they appear to.

## Why the Fantasy Persists

For people with high pattern-recognition, strong cross-domain ability, and resistance to arbitrary hierarchy, the patron model is the only frame in which their skills seem correctly valued. The alternative — accepting that the market will undervalue them — requires a kind of mourning that many postpone indefinitely.
