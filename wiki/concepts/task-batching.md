Grouping similar tasks together and completing them in a single time block, reducing cognitive overhead and context-switching costs.

Context-switching is expensive. Shifting from email to code to a meeting to social media to a spreadsheet fragments attention and increases errors. [[Task Batching]] reduces this cost by clustering similar work.

**Examples of batching:**
- All administrative tasks in one 90-minute block (email, invoicing, filing)
- Multiple interviews or customer calls back-to-back rather than scattered through the week
- A dedicated hour for kitchen prep rather than five 10-minute cooking sessions
- Writing work grouped together (emails, reports, drafts) rather than interspersed with other activities

Batching works synergistically with [[Time Blocking]] — the calendar reserves a block for a batch. It also supports [[Task Prioritization]]: batched Maintenance Tasks don't distract from Big Wins; they have their own scheduled window.

The cognitive principle is "set-up costs": the mental overhead of starting a task type is high (gathering context, switching modes), but the marginal cost of continuing is low. Batching amortizes set-up across multiple similar tasks.

Effectiveness depends on realistic grouping. Some work (deep coding, strategic thinking) cannot be batched with administrative tasks. Other work (admin, communication) batches naturally. The goal is reducing fragmentation where cognitive cost matters most.
