The things that actually produce aliveness — not as a performance of ambition, not what sounds good, but what is named directly in an unguarded moment:

- Building with LLMs and AI
- Ethical technology
- Worker co-operatives
- Being one's own boss
- Teaching
- Writing

These are not safe wants. They are structurally difficult, poorly incentivised by current markets, and in some cases require conditions that feel absent — nobody funds a non-profit trying to take down big tech. The author states this not as self-pity but as an honest assessment. The game is rigged against these wants.

The tension is not between wanting these things and wanting something easier. It is between wanting them and believing they are achievable. When that gap gets too large, wanting itself becomes painful — it produces longing without a path. The response is a kind of defensive withdrawal from the want itself: *I don't want it, because I know I can't have it.* This is distinct from not caring. The energy is still there. It shows up as anger at systems that foreclose these possibilities, as grief at their loss, and as genuine aliveness when anything adjacent to them is encountered — even just working with LLMs in a conventional employment context.

The authentic wants don't disappear. They go underground.

## Scale and Freedom

A related and distinct want: not just *what* to work on but at *what scale* and with *what freedom*. Corporate executive comfort — six-figure salary, a house in 50 years — does not meet the underlying need. The want is for billionaire-CEO-level autonomy and resource independence, the kind that permits entirely self-directed work. Not because wealth is the goal, but because that level of freedom is the only frame that feels compatible with actually pursuing authentic work without constraint. Accepting anything less registers as a kind of death. When death is already the background condition (see [[Depression and Ideation]]), the risk calculus of aiming this high stops feeling irrational.
