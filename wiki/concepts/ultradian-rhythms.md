Natural cycles of focus and recovery within a single day, typically lasting 90–120 minutes. Working with these rhythms rather than against them improves sustained performance and prevents burnout.

Research suggests humans have natural cycles of alertness and fatigue that repeat roughly every 90 minutes throughout the day. During the "focus" phase, energy and concentration are high; during the "recovery" phase, attention naturally dips. Many productivity systems fight this biology, trying to push through fatigue. The alternative is to honor the rhythm.

**Working with ultradian rhythms** means:
- Scheduling 90-minute [[Time Blocking]] sessions for deep work during peak alertness
- Taking 15–20 minute breaks between blocks rather than pushing for 6–8 hour stretches
- Using breaks strategically: short walks, light physical activity, meals, or rest rather than checking email
- Adjusting the schedule based on personal rhythm (some people peak in the morning, others in late afternoon)

The practice directly supports the [[Perfect Week Framework]], which emphasizes **taking breaks** as a non-negotiable component of sustainable productivity. Rest is not laziness; it is physiologically necessary for continued focus.

Ultradian rhythms interact with broader circadian rhythms (the 24-hour sleep-wake cycle) and seasonal patterns, but they operate within the day with measurable effect on energy, mood, and output quality.
