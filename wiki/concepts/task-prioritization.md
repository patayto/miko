A systematic approach to identifying and focusing on the work that matters most, preventing both task paralysis and low-impact busy work.

Task prioritization uses a **three-tier hierarchy**:

**Big Wins** are the 2–3 major accomplishments targeted for a given day. Each Big Win represents a significant topic or area of focus (e.g., "job application," "design new feature," "deep project work"). By limiting Big Wins to just 2–3 per day, the system creates realistic focus and measurable daily progress. Over a week, this compounds meaningfully.

**Maintenance Tasks** are recurring, necessary activities that keep life functional: laundry, email triage, admin work, meal prep, etc. These are scheduled but not elevated to Big Win status—they are acknowledged as necessary, time-allocated, and batched where possible, rather than allowed to fragment the day.

**Micro-tasks** are quick wins under 5 minutes: filing documents, sending a short message, tidying a shelf. These fill gaps and provide psychological wins when larger tasks stall.

The hierarchy prevents two failure modes: the paralysis of trying to do everything (mitigated by the 2–3 limit), and the illusion of productivity from only handling small items.

Effective prioritization works with [[Time Blocking]] to map Big Wins into protected calendar slots, and with [[Completion Psychology]] to reinforce follow-through. The system is flexible: some days will be 80% Maintenance (deep clean day), others 80% growth work. The [[Perfect Week Framework]] suggests balancing effort across a week rather than demanding daily perfection.
