The experience of having worked extraordinarily hard to achieve something — knowing the real cost of that work — and still being unable to enjoy it, because awareness of structural inequality poisons the pleasure.

The author's position is genuinely complex and deserves to be held in that complexity. Serious material and social disadvantages: Northern Ireland, gay, two decades of trauma, a history of physical and sexual abuse, years of social isolation, a teenage self locked in a bedroom grinding through 13 A* GCSEs (against an average of 6) and 6 A-levels (against an average of 3), sacrificing summers, dreams, a wanted life on stage, to build a future through sheer effort. Real structural advantages too: white, male — however complicated those categories felt from the inside, from the position of a gay man told all his life he wasn't a real man.

When someone with significantly greater material and social privilege named the author's privilege on the axes of race and gender, they absorbed it completely. They haven't let go. Now they feel unable to enjoy what they earned without guilt — while watching peers who had far larger head starts carry their success with total ease of conscience, telling them to enjoy life's finer things, not to worry so much.

The double bind: consciousness of inequality makes equality harder to embody, not easier. Seeing privilege clearly tends to produce judgment of those who can't or won't see it. That judgment creates distance. Distance looks like the same superiority the author is trying to resist. They are aware of this. They are doing it even in the act of noticing it.

The paradox at the centre: the people who got more, feel they deserve it. The person who got less, feels they deserve less.
