Passive suicidal ideation that is chronic rather than acute: a stable background thought of not-existing, not arising from sudden crisis but from accumulated exhaustion with the structural conditions of being alive.

This is distinct from active suicidal risk. The person is not acutely planning or imminently at risk. Rather, they have resigned themselves to the fact of their own death—estimated at 15-20 years in the future, once certain obligations (caring for aging parents, etc.) are discharged. The thought is not "I should die now" but "I understand I will die, and I am not grieving that timeline."

## The Functional Problem

Passive ideation creates a specific psychological bind: the person has no immediate escape route and no motivation to invest in long-term futures they won't care about inhabiting. This collapses the horizon of meaning-making. Short-term engagement feels pointless if the long-term trajectory is irrelevant. Yet the person is obliged to survive the interim—to show up to work, maintain relationships, not actively harm themselves—without the ordinary reward structures (hope, gratification, narrative purpose) that sustain most people.

The anger and bitterness often accompanying this state are rational responses to that bind, not symptoms of depression in the treatable sense. Therapy can help with the ideation itself, but it cannot change the structural facts that make the ideation feel realistic.

## Gratitude as Impossible

A person experiencing chronic ideation often cannot access gratitude for being alive. Life is not experienced as a gift. This creates a secondary layer of alienation: they watch others receive and give thanks for existence, and cannot locate that capacity in themselves. This is sometimes interpreted as depression (treatable), when it may be closer to a differing fundamental assessment of existence.

## The Four-Path Frame

When ideation is chronic and depression structural, the person may map out their options explicitly: end it, continue as-is, change the self (habits, cognition), or change the situation. Each path has a different cost and risk profile. The person cannot pretend the first option isn't there — naming it as the "final option" held in reserve is part of what makes the others feel bearable. Crucially, the same advisors who dismiss "change the situation" as too risky often contradict themselves elsewhere (e.g., claiming individual action matters). Detecting this hypocrisy is part of what maintains the person's distrust of outside guidance.

## Shooting for the Moon as Rational

When death feels like the background condition, probabilistic reasoning about career and ambition shifts. The small chance of achieving an extraordinary goal is not much worse than the certainty of a comfortable life that feels meaningless. Risk tolerance expands — not recklessly, but logically. If the alternatives are "die anyway" or "try the impossible," attempting the impossible becomes reasonable. This reframe lets the person act against conventional advice without feeling irrational.

The sustained high-intensity emotional baseline described in [[Emotional Dysregulation]] compounds structural depression: when every provocation hits at full force and recovery is slow, the cumulative load is significantly heavier. [[Sensory Sensitivity]] adds further ambient cost. Together these may contribute to the exhaustion that underlies chronic ideation.
