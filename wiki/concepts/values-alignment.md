Ensuring that daily decisions, priorities, and actions reflect one's core values, creating internal coherence and reducing decision fatigue.

A productivity system that ignores values becomes mechanically efficient but spiritually hollow. [[Values Alignment]] anchors the [[Perfect Week Framework]] by asking: what matters most? and does my schedule reflect that?

Core [[Values]] emerge from reflection and lived experience. They might include honesty, humility, perseverance, resilience, flexibility, and humour. Once articulated, they serve as a filter for decisions: a task aligned with core values deserves a Big Win slot; work that contradicts values creates invisible friction even if it appears productive.

**In practice:**
- Regular review of planned work against articulated values
- Noticing when tasks feel misaligned (a sign to reconsider or reframe them)
- Using values as tiebreakers when priorities conflict
- [[Shadow Work]] — examining blind spots, defensive patterns, and values gaps

Misalignment shows up as procrastination, low energy, resentment, or the sense that "I'm busy but not doing what matters." Alignment creates momentum even in difficult work.

The [[Perfect Week Framework]] treats values alignment not as nice-to-have philosophy but as a practical productivity tool. A week organized around personal values compounds more effectively than the same week in service of externally-imposed goals.

**Authentic wants (named directly):** building with LLMs and AI, ethical technology, worker co-operatives, being one's own boss, teaching, writing. These are documented more fully in [[Authentic Wants]]. The relationship between values alignment and authentic wants is not simple: a person can know their values clearly and still be unable to act on them — if the world seems to foreclose them, or if the internal [[Motivational Conflict|conflict between selves]] overrides the knowledge. Values alignment as a *productivity tool* assumes a relatively unified agent. When the split is deep enough, the tool doesn't reach the problem.
