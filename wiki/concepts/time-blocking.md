A scheduling technique that allocates specific calendar blocks of time to particular categories of work or life activities, creating boundaries and protecting focus.

In [[Time Blocking]], the calendar becomes a commitment device. Rather than maintaining an open task list and hoping to find time, blocks are reserved: 90 minutes for deep work, 30 minutes for email, 2 hours for a hobby project, a full day for a project sprint. Each block is treated as an appointment with the work itself.

Effective time blocking requires:
- **Realistic estimates** — building in buffer and acknowledging that tasks often take longer than anticipated
- **Task coupling** — batching similar work into blocks (e.g., all administrative tasks in one afternoon block) to reduce context switching
- **Sacred blocks** — protecting certain times (deep work in the morning, break time after focus sessions) from other demands
- **Flexibility** — distinguishing between critical blocks (must not move) and flexible slots that can shift if priorities change

Time blocking pairs naturally with [[Ultradian Rhythms]], which suggests natural cycles of focus and recovery lasting roughly 90 minutes. A schedule might alternate focused work blocks with shorter recovery breaks, rather than pushing for 8-hour stretches.

The practice combats the fragmentation that kills productivity: notifications, context-switching, and the anxiety of an unbounded task list. By making time explicit and bounded, it supports [[Task Prioritization]] and the [[2-3 Big Wins]] approach central to the [[Perfect Week Framework]].
