Sensory sensitivity describes atypical processing of sensory input — either heightened reactivity (hypersensitivity) or reduced reactivity (hyposensitivity) — across one or more modalities. Both ends of the spectrum can be present in the same person, in different modalities.

**Tactile hypersensitivity** is among the most common presentations: intolerance of certain textures (velvet, moss), ill-fitting or tight clothing, or clothing tags. The sensation is not merely uncomfortable but can trigger panic or a need to immediately resolve the source of the stimulus.

**Auditory hypersensitivity** frequently targets sounds with particular acoustic qualities: repetitive mouth sounds (chewing, lip-smacking), delicate impact sounds (clinking cutlery, glassware), and mechanical sounds with an irregular or pulsing character (fluorescent light buzz and strobe). These are often social sounds generated by other people and therefore difficult to avoid or escape.

**Visual sensitivity** centres on lighting quality — bright, cold, or fluorescent light in particular. The discomfort is both the light intensity and, when fluorescent, the flicker rate.

**Thermal hyposensitivity** (under-sensitivity to cold) is less commonly discussed but equally valid: consistently not perceiving temperatures that peers experience as uncomfortable, leading to no coat in cold weather without conscious awareness that the temperature warrants one.

Sensory sensitivity is not simply a matter of preference. Stimuli that cross the threshold trigger genuine stress responses — panic, shutdown, or need to escape — rather than mild discomfort. This affects choices about environments, clothing, social settings, and daily routine in ways that can appear idiosyncratic to others.

Sensory sensitivity is one of the recognised criteria in [[ASD]] assessment, and overlaps with [[Social Communication Differences]] (sensory overload can impair social processing) and [[Emotional Dysregulation]] (sensory triggers can initiate emotional flooding).
