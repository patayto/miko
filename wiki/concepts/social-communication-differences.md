Social communication differences refer to a pattern in which a person processes social interaction differently from the neurotypical norm — not through lack of interest, but through a distinct set of channels and priorities.

A central feature is reliance on the explicit content of speech rather than paralinguistic signals. Tone of voice, facial expression, and body language carry large amounts of social information, but when those channels are not processed fluently, the spoken words become the primary — sometimes only — input. This creates predictable failure modes: irony, sarcasm, indirect requests, and social hints are missed not because they were ignored but because the signal was never decoded.

The difficulty compounds in unstructured social settings, where there is no topic or activity to anchor conversation. Structured contexts (discussing a film after watching it together, collaborative games with clear rules) provide an external scaffold that compensates for reduced implicit social fluency. Large gatherings offer a different kind of relief: the social obligation is diffuse, and withdrawal is socially invisible.

Maintaining long-term relationships presents a specific challenge as novelty decreases. Early relationships are often sustained by new shared experiences; as those diminish and conversation defaults to open-ended check-ins ("how are you? what have you been up to?"), the demand for implicit social competence rises and the relationship becomes harder to sustain.

Social communication differences often co-occur with [[Sensory Sensitivity]], [[Hyperfixation]], and [[Emotional Dysregulation]], and are one of the core criteria considered during [[ASD]] assessment.
